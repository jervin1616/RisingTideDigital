How Much Does a Website Cost for a Small Business in Wilmington, NC?

April 3, 2026  •  By Joseph — Rising Tide Digital

Most small business owners in Wilmington don't know what a website should actually cost — and that uncertainty gets exploited regularly. You call a marketing agency, they quote you $5,000 for a five-page site, and you either pay it because you don't know better or you walk away and put the project off for another year.

Neither outcome is good for your business. A website is the single most important piece of your online presence, and you should be able to get a solid one without paying agency prices for a contractor's budget.

This post gives you straight answers. Here's what a professional small business website actually costs in the Wilmington area — broken down by tier, by what's included, and by what actually drives the price up versus what agencies use to justify markups that have nothing to do with your project.

The honest price range for a small business website

For a custom-built, mobile-ready website for a small business in Wilmington, NC, here's the realistic range:

Tier Price Best for
Starter $800 flat Solo operators, new businesses, anyone who needs a clean online presence fast — up to 5 pages
Growth $1,800 flat Established small businesses needing more pages, a contact form, and local SEO — 6 to 10 pages
Authority $3,500 flat Businesses ready to dominate local search with a full-featured site — 11 to 20 pages
Custom Contact for quote 20+ pages — fully scoped to your requirements

These are flat-rate figures — meaning you know the number before work begins, and it doesn't change unless the scope does. That's how it should work. Hourly billing for a website project is a setup for scope creep and invoice shock, and you should be skeptical of any designer who won't give you a fixed price upfront.

What drives the price up (and what doesn't matter)

A few factors legitimately add to the cost of a website:

  • Number of pages. A 10-page site takes more time than a 3-page site. Simple as that.
  • Custom copywriting. If you want someone to write your About page, service descriptions, and SEO content from scratch, that's real work worth paying for. If you're providing your own copy, the price drops.
  • E-commerce. Selling products online — even just a handful of them — adds setup time, payment integration, and testing. Budget for it.
  • Booking integrations. If you want clients to schedule appointments directly from your site, that requires third-party tool setup and custom embedding.
  • Logo design. If you're starting from scratch with no brand assets, a custom logo is a separate deliverable and should be priced accordingly.

What doesn't need to cost extra: WordPress plugin licenses, monthly platform subscriptions, "design fees" layered on top of development fees, or a three-meeting discovery process before anyone opens a code editor. A good web designer can scope your project in a single conversation and get to work.

What to watch out for when hiring a web designer in Wilmington

Not every red flag is obvious. Here are a few patterns worth knowing:

  • Hourly billing with no cap. If a designer charges by the hour with no fixed ceiling, you have no idea what you'll owe until it's over. Ask for a flat rate or a not-to-exceed cap.
  • Platform lock-in. If someone builds your site on Wix, Squarespace, or a proprietary platform, you don't truly own it. When you stop paying their subscription, your site disappears. Insist on owning the code and being able to host it anywhere. (We break down all the template trade-offs in detail if you're weighing that route.)
  • No contract. Any legitimate web designer uses a contract. It protects both sides. If someone's working on a handshake, that's a problem waiting to happen.
  • Vague timelines. "A few weeks" isn't a timeline. You should get a clear milestone map: when will the first draft be ready, when do revisions happen, when does the site go live.

In Wilmington especially, there are a lot of freelancers who've built a site or two and call themselves designers. That's not necessarily bad — but ask to see real work, ask who will answer the phone if something breaks after launch, and get the agreement in writing.

What you should expect for $800 (the Starter tier)

A Starter-tier website is not a cut-rate, corner-cut product. It's a focused build for businesses that know what they need: a homepage that communicates clearly, a services or about page, a contact form, and a site that looks sharp on mobile.

Here's what an $800 Starter site includes:

  • Up to 5 pages of custom HTML/CSS (no page-builder junk)
  • Fully mobile-responsive design
  • Contact form with email delivery
  • On-page SEO basics (title tags, meta descriptions, heading structure)
  • Fast load times — no bloated plugins dragging down your Google ranking
  • You own the code, the domain, and all files at the end
  • Up to 3 rounds of revisions included

Who is this right for? A handyman or contractor who needs a professional online presence. A cleaning company that wants to show up in Google when someone searches "house cleaning Wilmington NC." A new business that needs something credible before they start outbound marketing. A Starter site does that job well.

What it's not: a full e-commerce store, a booking-integrated platform, or a site with more than 5 pages. Those are Growth or Authority builds, and they're priced accordingly. Add-ons like copywriting ($500), logo design ($300), and booking integration ($150) are available but not included by default in any tier.
