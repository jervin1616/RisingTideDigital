Website Templates: Pros, Cons, and When They Actually Make Sense for Small Businesses in Wilmington, NC

April 30, 2026  •  By Joseph — Rising Tide Digital

Note: Pricing and packages have been updated. See our current services page for the latest.

Templates have gotten a lot better. Ten years ago, a Wix or Squarespace site practically announced itself — boxy layouts, slow loads, and a certain generic sameness that told visitors they weren't dealing with a serious operation. That's less true today. The platforms have matured, the design quality has gone up, and for a certain type of business, a well-chosen template can absolutely get the job done.

But templates have real limits too, and the marketing around them tends to hide those limits pretty aggressively. "Build your site in minutes!" is a promise designed to get you to sign up for a subscription — not to help you figure out whether that's actually the right call for your business.

So here's a straight look at both sides, without the spin.

What templates do well

PROSpeed to launch. If you need something online fast — like this week — a template wins. You pick a layout, drop in your content and photos, and publish. A custom build takes more time because it's being made specifically for you. Templates front-load the design work so you can move faster.

PROLow upfront cost. Most template platforms charge $15–$50/month with no large upfront fee. For a business that's genuinely not sure yet whether a website will pay off — someone testing a new service or launching a side project — that low barrier is a real advantage.

PRODIY-friendly. If you're comfortable on a computer and willing to spend a few hours, you can manage a template site yourself. Changing photos, updating hours, adding a new service page — these are all doable without calling anyone. That matters if you want control and don't want to pay for minor updates.

PROBuilt-in hosting and maintenance. Squarespace, Wix, and Shopify handle the server, the security patches, and the uptime. You don't need to think about those things. For non-technical owners, that peace of mind is worth something.

PRODecent mobile experience out of the box. The major platforms have invested heavily in responsive design. Most templates look reasonable on a phone without you having to do anything special.

Where templates fall short

CONYou don't own anything. This is the one that most business owners don't fully grasp until it's too late. Your Squarespace site doesn't belong to you — it belongs to Squarespace. The moment you stop paying the monthly fee, the site goes dark. You can't take the code and move it to a cheaper host. You can't hand it to a developer to build on top of. You're renting, not owning.

CONPlatform lock-in. Moving off a template platform is painful. Your content is trapped in their system. If the platform raises prices, changes features, or gets acquired, your options are limited. Migration usually means rebuilding from scratch anyway — so the "cheap to start" math changes when you factor in switching costs down the road.

CONGeneric appearance. Every template starts from a shared design. You can change colors and swap photos, but the underlying structure is the same one thousands of other businesses are using. In competitive local markets — like Wilmington's contractor and service industry — looking identical to your competitors isn't neutral. It's a missed opportunity to stand out.

CONSEO limitations. Template platforms have improved their SEO tools, but they still carry overhead that custom-coded sites don't. Bloated page builders, extra JavaScript, and limited control over technical SEO elements (like structured data, canonical tags, and load performance) put a ceiling on how well your site can rank. When you're competing for "HVAC repair Wilmington NC," that ceiling matters.

CONPerformance bloat. Templates are built to be flexible for everyone, which means they load a lot of code you don't use. Core Web Vitals — the performance metrics Google factors into rankings — tend to be worse on template platforms than on lean custom-coded sites. Slower sites lose visitors and rank lower.

CONMonthly costs compound. That $23/month Squarespace plan sounds cheap until you're three years in and have paid $828 for a site you still don't own. A custom-built site at a flat rate often pays for itself in two to three years compared to a template subscription — and you have an asset to show for it. So when does a template actually make sense?

Honestly, there are situations where starting with a template is the right call:

  • You're testing a business idea and aren't ready to invest in something permanent yet.
  • You need something live this week and have zero budget for professional help.
  • You plan to manage all your own content and updates indefinitely.
  • Your industry isn't competitive online and you just need a basic contact page.

But if you're an established business — a contractor, a cleaning company, a trade service, a local shop — trying to show up in local search and convert visitors into customers, a template is going to hold you back in ways that become more costly the longer you stay on one. If you're a contractor specifically, read our post on whether tradespeople in Wilmington actually need a website — it covers the business case in more depth.

The bottom line for Wilmington small businesses

Templates are a starting point, not a destination. They're fine for getting something online quickly, but they trade long-term leverage for short-term convenience. You don't own the site, you can't fully optimize it for search, and you're paying every month for the privilege.

A professionally built custom site — priced fairly and built clean — gives you a permanent asset, better performance, and a look that's actually yours. For most established local businesses, the math favors going custom from the start rather than migrating off a template platform later.
